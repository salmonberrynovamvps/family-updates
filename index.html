Background — The Law That Was Supposed to Fix This

To understand what's happening to us, it helps to know what was supposed to prevent it.

When COVID hit, Congress authorized mortgage forbearance — a temporary pause on payments — through the CARES Act. This wasn't forgiveness; the money was still owed, but families could stabilize without losing their homes. The VA had a program called the VA Partial Claim Program that was supposed to help veteran families get caught up after forbearance by deferring the missed payments. That program ended. Then the VA created a second relief program called VASP (VA Servicing Purchase Program) to catch families who still needed help.

Here's where it broke: The VA unexpectedly terminated VASP on May 1, 2025 — with only 8 days notice. Veteran families who were in the middle of that process, including us, had the rug swept out from under them. Our mortgage servicer, Freedom Mortgage, held our complete application for over three months without ever submitting it to the VA, so we missed the deadline through no fault of our own.

The scale of this crisis: When NPR first started reporting, the number of affected veteran families was around 40,000. As the VA's program failures cascaded — the first relief program ending, VASP being terminated with 8 days notice, the implementation gap after H.R. 1815 — that number grew to 70,000–80,000 families. Current estimates are now approaching 90,000 veteran families nationwide who have been caught in this mess. We are one of them.

Congress recognized this was a disaster. In July 2025, they passed the VA Home Loan Program Reform Act (H.R. 1815), which created a new permanent partial claim option. Everyone thought that fixed the problem.

But here's what actually happened: The VA didn't implement the new law in time, and there was no regulation or enforcement ensuring that mortgage companies didn't take advantage of the gap. Freedom Mortgage and other servicers were free to keep foreclosing on veteran families during the months between the law passing and the VA actually standing up the new program. That's exactly what happened to us — and to tens of thousands of other families.

What's Happened Since the Last Update (Feb 27)

NPR Investigation

NPR's investigative journalists came to our house on March 9 for a photo shoot. They've been investigating VA mortgage servicing failures for years, and they are now preparing a standalone article specifically about our case. The first article in this series is expected to publish next week — we'll share it when it's out.

NPR is directly contacting the Secretary of the VA, Freedom Mortgage, and congressional offices as part of their reporting. But they've also gone beyond just reporting — they've used their contacts to connect us directly with key people who can help, including the House Veterans Affairs Committee staff and the King County VA Director who works with Senator Murray's office. That's not something journalists typically do; they usually maintain separation between reporting and advocacy. The fact that they're making these connections on our behalf — using their own professional relationships to open doors for us — speaks to how seriously they view this case and how clearly the evidence supports what we've been saying. We're grateful for it.

Why this matters: National media attention creates public accountability — agencies and companies respond differently when reporters are asking questions on the record. And NPR actively connecting us with congressional and government contacts has dramatically accelerated our ability to reach the people who can actually intervene.

House Veterans Affairs Committee

This is the congressional committee in the U.S. House of Representatives that has oversight authority over the VA — meaning they can investigate VA actions, question VA leadership, and pressure agencies to act. NPR connected us with Halle Sarkisian, the staff director for the Subcommittee on Economic Opportunity (under Chairman Mike Bost). We submitted privacy releases and our full case documentation. Halle said she would relay our case directly to Freedom Mortgage and report back. We've asked the committee to request that the VA — which currently owns our house — direct its property management contractor to pause any move-out enforcement or eviction proceedings while investigations are active.

Rep. Baumgartner's office (our district representative) has two staffers — Jane Scarborough and Isabela Schandlbauer — who have been on every single call, meeting, and email with Halle and the House committee. They're working jointly together, not separately. Having our district representative's staff actively involved alongside the national committee means there's both local and federal-level accountability happening in real time.

Why this matters: Congressional committee staff have direct lines to agency leadership that individual citizens don't have. When a committee asks the VA questions, the VA has to answer. This isn't a suggestion box — it's congressional oversight.

Senator Murray's Office

NPR also connected us with Joseph Najmolhoda, the King County Director of Veterans Affairs, who also serves as Senator Patty Murray's VSO (Veteran Service Organization) liaison. He personally filed a congressional inquiry with the VA on our behalf back on Feb 27. We sent him updated case documents and the same eviction-pause request on March 13. Senator Murray sits on the Senate Veterans Affairs Committee, which gives her office additional weight when asking the VA to review a case.

Why this matters: A congressional inquiry means the VA has to formally respond — they can't just ignore it or let it sit in a queue.

VA Office of Inspector General (OIG)

The OIG is the VA's internal watchdog — an independent office that investigates fraud, waste, abuse, and employee misconduct within the VA. We filed our complaint on February 20 (Reference #2026-16691).

When we contacted them and filed the report, they were immediately responsive and took us seriously — which was honestly a relief after months of getting the runaround from other VA offices. It felt like we were finally talking to people who actually wanted to know what happened. The complaint is active, and we plan to follow up with them on Monday.

Why this matters: An open OIG investigation means the VA knows its own watchdog is looking at this. The OIG can issue findings that lead to disciplinary action against VA employees, policy changes, or referrals for further investigation — and it creates a permanent official record that can't be swept under the rug.

WA Attorney General — Military & Veteran Legal Assistance (OMVLA)

We filed a report with the WA Attorney General's OMVLA office, which provides legal assistance to military and veteran families. They confirmed receipt, reviewed our income (we qualify — about 184% of the federal poverty level), and referred us to Northwest Justice Project for free legal representation. We're now following back up with OMVLA for next steps since NJP declined (more on that below).

WA Attorney General — Consumer Protection Division

We're also filing a separate complaint with the WA AG's Consumer Protection Division. This is different from the OMVLA veteran assistance — this targets Freedom Mortgage's deceptive business practices directly under Washington State law. Washington's Consumer Protection Act allows for treble damages (3x actual damages) up to $25,000 per violation, plus mandatory attorney fees. We have multiple documented violations.

This creates state-level regulatory pressure independently of our federal complaints. It's another direction the pressure is coming from.

Public Comment on VA Policy

When federal agencies create new rules or programs, they're required by law to accept public comments before finalizing them. The VA is currently writing the implementation handbook for the new partial claim program created by H.R. 1815 — the law described above that Congress passed to fix the foreclosure crisis after the VA unexpectedly ended the earlier relief programs.

We submitted a 16-page public comment with 12 specific recommendations, citing our case as a real-world example of what happens when these programs aren't applied in time and mortgage companies aren't held accountable during the gap.

Why this matters: Our comment is now part of the permanent federal regulatory record — VA staff, policymakers, and future researchers will see it. It also demonstrates that we're not just fighting for ourselves; we're contributing to the policy solution so this doesn't keep happening to other veteran families.

Additional filings we're preparing this week:

  • Senator Maria Cantwell's office — our other U.S. Senator, filing a constituent case so both WA senators are engaged

We're also requesting recordings of our phone calls with VA staff, including one where a VA supervisor acknowledged on a VA-recorded line that the VA "dropped the ball" on our case.

Each of these creates an additional layer of accountability. Agencies and companies respond to pressure, and right now we're building pressure from every direction we can.

The Retaliation Evidence

While combing through years of documentation to build our legal case, we identified what appears to be direct retaliation by Freedom Mortgage.

The sequence:

July 8, 2025: Jerrod emails the VA, explicitly calling Freedom Mortgage's conduct "predatory." The VA loan technician forwards that email to Freedom Mortgage the same day through official VA channels.

July 22, 2025: Fourteen days later, Freedom Mortgage refers our loan to a foreclosure attorney. This is the formal start of foreclosure proceedings — they made this decision before Congress passed the law that would have required them to evaluate us for relief first.

July 30, 2025: Eight days after Freedom Mortgage's foreclosure referral, Congress signs H.R. 1815 into law — the VA Home Loan Program Reform Act. This law would have required Freedom Mortgage to evaluate us under the new partial claim program before pursuing foreclosure. But Freedom Mortgage had already pulled the trigger.

The pattern: Veteran files complaint through VA channels → VA forwards complaint directly to the company the veteran is complaining about → company fast-tracks foreclosure 14 days later → Congress passes a law 8 days after that which would have protected the veteran, but the company already moved first. The documentation for all of these events is in our file. We've flagged this to NPR and to the House Veterans Affairs Committee.

The Hard Parts

We have no attorney.

Northwest Justice Project — Washington's free legal aid organization for foreclosure cases — declined our case on March 11. They said they can't take cases of this scope. A foreclosure attorney that NPR connected us with also declined and referred us back to NJP. Both dead ends. We have a list of private attorneys to start calling next week, and we're asking every contact we have for referrals. The WA Consumer Protection Act's mandatory attorney fees provision should make this case attractive for a private attorney on contingency (meaning they get paid from the outcome, not upfront). This is our most urgent gap right now.

VRM sent us a "Cash for Keys" offer.

VRM is Vendor Resource Management — the property management company the VA contracts with to handle properties it owns after foreclosure. They offered $3,500 to voluntarily move out by April 3. The catch: signing it permanently releases ALL of our legal rights and claims against every party — VA, Freedom Mortgage, the trustee, everyone. All of them. Forever. We are not signing it.

We sent an extension request asking for 90 days while investigations are active. Their letter itself said extensions could be requested, so we did. We're waiting to hear back from their legal team.

Important context on the April 3 date: This is not an eviction date. It's the deadline on a voluntary offer that we aren't accepting because it requires us to give up every legal claim we have. Even if they deny the extension, they would still have to go to Spokane County court and file a formal unlawful detainer (eviction) proceeding — that's a separate legal process with its own timeline. We obviously don't want that — it can move fast once filed, days to a week or so at each step — but no one is showing up April 3 to change our locks.

That said, we are preparing for the possibility that things could move quickly after that date, which is why we're sharing all of this now.

We've asked every congressional and committee contact to request that the VA (which owns the property) direct VRM to pause enforcement. The VA owns the house. They control VRM's contract. They can pause this whenever they choose to.

Jerrod's health is getting worse.

Jerrod is rated 100% Totally and Permanently Disabled (T&P TDIU) through the VA — service-connected TBI, seizure disorder, PTSD, functional neurological disorder, and autonomic dysfunction from two deployments to Afghanistan. That's not a number on paper; it shapes every part of daily life and what "accessible housing" actually means for our family.

Seizures are increasing — his medications have been doubled. They evaluated him for cardiac issues this week and ruled that out, which means the symptoms are neurological/TBI-based. More diagnostics and imaging are scheduled for next week. The stress and fear from the foreclosure is actively destabilizing his disability. This is worse than the last time we faced housing instability — he's more medically fragile now than he was in 2018–2019.

Our furnace was installed wrong and has been leaking carbon monoxide.

An HVAC tech confirmed on Thursday that the furnace — installed by a contractor about two years ago — was put in incorrectly, broke internal parts, and has been releasing carbon monoxide into the house this entire time. That furnace was paid for by Semper Fi & America's Fund (a national veteran service organization) as part of critical home repairs during our VA forbearance period. The same contractor also installed our AC wrong (pipes froze, had to come back). Replacement costs $6,800.

Before anyone panics: we're okay. We can't use the furnace, but we have two space heaters going in the living room with sheets and blankets up over all the doorways to keep the heat in. We actually had to turn the space heaters down because we started overheating — so as long as things stay like this, we're managing just fine while we figure out next steps with our caseworker at Semper Fi & America's Fund. It snowed again over the last 48 hours, but we're warm. Temperature regulation is medically important for Jerrod's conditions, and right now we've got it handled.

Our kid's school stability.

Our son has an active IEP (Individualized Education Program) through Spokane Public Schools. Keeping him in his current school through the end of this school year is a priority — any relocation has to account for that. He's already dealing with the stress of all this at home; pulling him out of school mid-year would compound it. If we have to move, we'll need to figure out transportation to keep his placement stable.

Why we didn't sound the alarm sooner.
